# Content Spine: Keynote Deck (28 slides, flexible 20-35 range)

The keynote spine below is the canonical structure for a 28-slide, ~21-minute keynote talk. Adjust slide count and narrative depth up or down for shorter (20-slide, ~15 min) or longer (35-slide, ~26 min) keynotes, maintaining the core arc: Hook, Thesis, Evidence, Stories, Implications, Counter-arguments, Call To Action.

---

## Slide 1 · Cover / Title

**Purpose**: Establish the speaker and the talk title in a single, compelling visual.

**Required elements**:
- Speaker name (first/last, centered or asymmetric)
- Talk title or thesis statement (one line, large type)
- Event/date or affiliation (small, secondary)
- One strong visual (background image, bold color, or minimalist shape)

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't list multiple subtitles or taglines.
- Don't include a logo so large it crowds the speaker name.
- Don't make the cover busy. It's a door, not a poster.

---

## Slide 2 · Hook / Opening Story or Stat

**Purpose**: Arrest attention in the first 30 seconds with a story beat, a striking statistic, or a provocative question. This is the most critical slide.

**Required elements**:
- One story opening, data point, or question that forces the audience to lean in
- Full-bleed image, bold background, or minimalist visual that reinforces the hook
- Minimal or no text overlay. Let the image and the speaker carry the moment

**What NOT to do**:
- Never start with "Hi, I'm X" or an agenda.
- Don't overload with text or multiple points.
- Don't mute the visual with unnecessary labels.
- Don't hedge or apologize ("So, um, I'm going to talk about...").

---

## Slide 3 · Thesis / The One Idea

**Purpose**: State the central claim of the talk in one sentence or phrase. Every story, example, and argument that follows supports this thesis.

**Required elements**:
- One clear statement of the talk's main idea
- Visual treatment: large type, minimal decoration, or a symbolic image
- NO explanation or breakdown yet. Save that for slides to come

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't turn the thesis into a bullet list.
- Don't hedge ("We're exploring the idea that...").
- Don't introduce counter-arguments here. That comes later.

---

## Slide 4 · Why This Matters Now

**Purpose**: Establish urgency and relevance. Why should the audience care at this moment in time?

**Required elements**:
- A data point, market shift, or cultural moment that creates urgency
- A trend chart, timeline, or visual that anchors the "now"
- Clear context: who does this affect, and why?

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't make it sound inevitable or doom-laden without proof.
- Don't say "the world is changing" without examples.
- Don't skip this. Without relevance, the thesis feels academic.

---

## Slides 5 to 10 · Argument & Evidence (6 slides, one idea each)

**Purpose**: Build the case for the thesis with specific arguments, each backed by data, logic, or narrative.

**Slide 5 · First Argument**:
- Argument name or framing (one line)
- Supporting data, chart, quote, or image
- One supporting point

**Slide 6 · Second Argument**:
- New argument, distinct idea
- Fresh visual (never repeat the same chart type)

**Slide 7 · Third Argument**:
- Continue building the case
- Maintain visual variety

**Slide 8 · Fourth Argument**:
- Deepen the logic
- Shift from abstract to concrete if possible

**Slide 9 · Fifth Argument**:
- Add nuance or a counterpoint that strengthens the main thesis
- Visual anchors the subtlety

**Slide 10 · Sixth Argument (or synthesis)**:
- Closing argument or synthesis slide
- Bridge to the story/example section

**Required elements per slide**:
- One clear argument or data point
- One visual: chart, image, or bold type treatment
- No bullet lists. One idea, expressed simply

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't make all six arguments equal weight; sequence them (simple to complex, or concrete to abstract).
- Don't repeat the same visual type (e.g., five line charts).
- Don't use images that don't directly reinforce the argument.

---

## Slides 11 to 18 · Stories / Examples / Proof (8 slides)

**Purpose**: Illustrate the argument with concrete, memorable examples. This is where the audience feels the thesis in their bones.

**Slide 11 · Story Setup / Cast**:
- A person, company, or situation that the story is about
- Portrait, logo, or scene-setting image
- No plot yet. Just the "who"

**Slide 12 · The Problem**:
- What the protagonist faced before the shift
- Data, dialogue, or emotional beat that sets the stakes

**Slide 13 · The Shift / Action**:
- What changed or what they tried
- The inflection point

**Slide 14 · The Outcome / Result**:
- What happened as a result
- Data, testimonial, or concrete change

**Slide 15 · Second Story Setup** (optional; can combine 11-14 as one 4-slide story, then repeat 15-18 for a second story):
- Another example to reinforce the thesis
- Different context, same principle

**Slide 16 · Second Story Problem**:
- Stakes for the second story

**Slide 17 · Second Story Action & Outcome** (compressed):
- The shift and result in one visual

**Slide 18 · Proof or Testimonial**:
- A quote, stat, or logos of customers/partners affirming the thesis
- Or a full-bleed image of the outcome

**Required elements per slide**:
- One narrative beat or piece of evidence
- One visual: photo, quote in large type, logo, or diagram
- Clear emotional or logical progression

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't tell two stories on one slide.
- Don't use abstract icons or placeholder images.
- Don't rush the narrative. Let each beat breathe.

---

## Slides 19 to 23 · Implications / What To Do (5 slides)

**Purpose**: Zoom out. Having proven the thesis with evidence and stories, what does it mean for the audience? What should they do, think, or believe differently?

**Slide 19 · The Shift in Mindset**:
- Reframe the listener's perspective
- Visual: metaphor, diagram, or large type

**Slide 20 · First Implication**:
- One concrete way the thesis changes behavior, strategy, or belief
- Who does it affect?

**Slide 21 · Second Implication**:
- Another domain or audience segment
- Different visual treatment

**Slide 22 · Third Implication**:
- Scope it wide or deep
- Summarize the pattern

**Slide 23 · The Upside / Opportunity**:
- If the audience embraces this, what becomes possible?
- Aspirational visual (not cheesy)

**Required elements**:
- Each implication is actionable or intellectually clear
- Progression from narrow to broad, or vice versa
- Visuals that shift the tone slightly. Moving from "proof" to "possibility"

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't turn implications into abstract philosophy.
- Don't repeat the argument slides.
- Don't make this section feel like a lecture. It's a conversation.

---

## Slides 24 to 26 · Counter-arguments & Nuance (3 slides)

**Purpose**: Acknowledge limits, risks, and alternative views. Credibility comes from intellectual honesty.

**Slide 24 · The Biggest Objection**:
- What would a smart skeptic say?
- State it clearly, without strawmanning

**Slide 25 · Why It Matters, Even Given The Objection**:
- The objection is real, but here's why the thesis still holds
- Data, logic, or nuance that refines the argument

**Slide 26 · The Trade-off or Caveat**:
- There's no silver bullet; name the costs or limits
- Visual that humanizes the nuance (not a downer, but honest)

**Required elements**:
- Real objections, not invented ones
- Transparent reasoning, not defensive rhetoric
- Respect for the alternative view

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't weakly dismiss objections.
- Don't use this section to re-argue the thesis.
- Don't end on a negative note. Transition smoothly to the CTA.

---

## Slide 27 · Call To Action / Next Steps

**Purpose**: Concrete ask. What should the audience do, starting today or this week?

**Required elements**:
- One clear, specific action
- Audience segment it applies to (everyone, founders, investors, etc.)
- How to get started (link, email, date, or simple next step)
- Visual: could be a resource, a QR code, a website URL, or a symbolic image

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't ask for three things. (Pick one.)
- Don't make the ask vague ("think differently about this").
- Don't skip a CTA. Even if it's just "share this with one person," have one.

---

## Slide 28 · Thank You / Contact / Closing Image

**Purpose**: Land gracefully. Leave the audience with a final image and a way to reach you.

**Required elements**:
- Speaker name and/or title
- One contact method (email, website, social handle)
- Optional: a thank-you message or parting image
- Visual: full-bleed image, bold color, or minimalist treatment (depends on speaker brand)

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't end with a generic "Questions?" slide.
- Don't make contact info so small it's unreadable.
- Don't contradict the opening visual. Close with aesthetic consistency.

---

## Hard constraints

- **Slide count**: 20 to 35 slides (default 28). A 28-slide keynote is ~21 minutes at 45 seconds per slide.
- **45 seconds per slide rule**: Do not assume listeners will read long text. Each slide should consume ~45 seconds of speaking time, including audience reaction time.
- **No emoji on any slide**: All decorative symbols use the icon library (`deck-builder/references/icon-library.md`). Emoji are forbidden.
- **No slide is all text**: Every slide has a visual anchor, image, chart, icon, large type treatment, or design shape. Bullet-list-only slides fail.
- **One idea per slide**: Each slide carries one main thought, one narrative beat, or one data point. No slide is a dense paragraph or unrelated list.
- **Opening hook mandatory**: Slide 2 (immediately after cover) must hook the audience. Never start with "Hi, I'm X" or an agenda. The hook is the speaker's job, and the slide is the set design for it.
