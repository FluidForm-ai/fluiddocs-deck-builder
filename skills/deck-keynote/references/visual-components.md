# Visual Components: Keynote Deck Patterns

Keynote is the most visual of all deck types. Every slide must anchor the speaker's voice with a clear visual statement. Below are the canonical visual patterns for each slide or slide group in the content spine. Use these patterns to ensure variety, coherence, and impact across the 28-slide arc.

---

## Slide 1 · Cover / Title

**Pattern A: Full-Bleed Background**
- Use when: Speaker wants an immersive opening visual
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed photograph or gradient background, 1440×810, no chrome
  - Speaker name and talk title in large type (240 to 300pt for title, 120pt for name)
  - Text color contrasts strongly with background (white on dark, dark on light)
  - Optional: event/date in 48pt, secondary position (bottom corner or top right)

**Pattern B: Bold Color & Large Type**
- Use when: Minimalist aesthetic or personal brand favors clarity over image
- Visual structure:
  - Solid background color (brand-forward or high-contrast)
  - Title and speaker name in large type, centered or asymmetric
  - No imagery; typography carries the visual weight
  - White space breathing room essential

**Pattern C: Logo/Brand Icon + Type**
- Use when: Speaker's personal brand or organization is central
- Visual structure:
  - Logo or monogram in 200 to 300pt (not larger than name/title)
  - Name and title arranged asymmetrically around the mark
  - Subtle background or white space for balance
  - Avoid logo clutter, one primary mark only

---

## Slide 2 · Hook / Opening Story or Stat

**Pattern A: Full-Bleed Image with Minimal Text Overlay**
- Use when: The image itself is the hook (striking photograph, visual metaphor)
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed photograph or illustration, 1440×810
  - Optional text overlay in large type (120pt+) if a statistic or one-word prompt is needed
  - Text positioned bottom-third or asymmetric, high contrast
  - Let the image do most of the work; speaker will narrate

**Pattern B: Large-Type Hook Statement**
- Use when: The statistic or quote IS the hook (no image needed)
- Visual structure:
  - Bold background color or subtle gradient
  - Single number or quote in 180 to 240pt, centered or left-aligned
  - Example: "42% of founders burn out" or "There's no innovation without failure"
  - Minimal decoration; the number or phrase is the visual

**Pattern C: Provocative Visual with Icon or Graphic**
- Use when: A metaphor or graphic accent enhances the opening tension
- Visual structure:
  - Bold background or subtle image
  - Icon (from icon library) or simple shape at 300 to 400pt, semi-transparent or solid
  - Hook text in 120 to 160pt, integrated with the graphic
  - Example: a rising arrow icon with "The only way is..."

---

## Slide 3 · Thesis / The One Idea

**Pattern A: Single Word or Phrase at Large Scale**
- Use when: The thesis is one concept or term
- Visual structure:
  - One word or short phrase (2 to 4 words max) in 240 to 300pt, bold
  - Background color or subtle gradient
  - Minimal or no other text
  - Example: "Constraints breed creativity" or "Platform > Product"

**Pattern B: Symbolic Image + Title**
- Use when: A visual metaphor anchors the thesis
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed or large background image that symbolizes the idea
  - Thesis text overlaid in 120 to 160pt, high contrast, bottom or center
  - Image might be abstract, metaphorical, or literal
  - Example: A lighthouse with "Clarity is the compass"

**Pattern C: Centered Quote or Statement**
- Use when: The thesis reads as a narrative statement
- Visual structure:
  - Centered text, 200pt+, serif or sans-serif (match brand)
  - Subtle background or white space
  - Optional: quotation marks in large decorative type (100pt)
  - Example: "The best startup ideas feel like obvious, in retrospect"

---

## Slides 5 to 10 · Argument & Evidence (6 slides, 2 to 3 patterns per slide)

**Pattern A: Data Visualization + Label**
- Use when: Argument is backed by a trend, comparison, or statistic
- Visual structure:
  - Chart (line, bar, scatter, or pie) at 600 to 800×400, centered or right-aligned
  - Argument title in 100pt, top or left
  - One supporting label or callout (48pt) near the chart
  - No more than one chart per slide; one data series preferred

**Pattern B: Large-Type Argument with Full-Bleed Background Image**
- Use when: The argument is more philosophical or narrative; image sets mood
- Visual structure:
  - Background image (photograph, illustration) at 30 to 50% opacity
  - Argument statement in 180 to 240pt, bold, centered or left-aligned
  - High text contrast; image is backdrop, not decoration
  - One supporting sentence (72pt) below argument, if needed

**Pattern C: Icon / Symbol + Supporting Narrative**
- Use when: Argument is a principle or capability (not data-heavy)
- Visual structure:
  - Icon from icon library, 300 to 400pt, centered or left-aligned
  - Argument title in 120pt next to or above icon
  - Supporting text (72pt) in 2 to 3 lines below
  - Example: Lightbulb icon with "Innovation requires experimentation"

**Pattern D: Side-by-Side Comparison (Before/After or This/That)**
- Use when: Argument contrasts two states or approaches
- Visual structure:
  - Split canvas 50/50 (left/right)
  - Left: one image or label (e.g., "The Old Way")
  - Right: contrasting image or label (e.g., "The New Way")
  - Argument title above or spanning both, 120 to 160pt
  - Bold visual difference between left and right

## Slides 11 to 18 · Stories / Examples / Proof (8 slides)

**Pattern A: Portrait or Company Logo + Story Beat**
- Use when: Slide introduces a person or organization
- Visual structure:
  - Portrait photograph (300×300 to 400×400) or company logo (200 to 300pt)
  - Name or company name in 100pt below
  - One-line context ("A healthcare founder" or "A Series A startup") in 72pt, gray or secondary color
  - Clean white or neutral background

**Pattern B: Full-Bleed Image Representing the Problem or Shift**
- Use when: Visual storytelling is stronger than text
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed or large photograph (600×800+) showing the moment, problem, or shift
  - Optional: one-line caption or label in 72 to 96pt, positioned bottom or corner
  - Let the audience absorb the visual; speaker carries narrative weight
  - Example: A founder at their desk (problem/challenge context)

**Pattern C: Quote or Testimonial in Large Type**
- Use when: The story's emotional or logical peak is a direct quote
- Visual structure:
  - Quote in 140 to 180pt, serif or sans-serif
  - Quotation marks in 100pt, decorative
  - Attribution (name, title) in 72pt below, smaller color
  - Background color or subtle image (20% opacity) to anchor the slide

**Pattern D: Data or Metric Representing Outcome**
- Use when: The story's result is measurable
- Visual structure:
  - One large number or metric in 240 to 300pt (e.g., "300% revenue growth", "+150 customers")
  - Label or context in 100pt above or below
  - Supporting detail (72pt) if needed
  - Background color or subtle chart representing the change

**Pattern E: Logos or Social Proof**
- Use when: Multiple case studies or proof points are shown together
- Visual structure:
  - 5 to 8 company logos arranged in a grid or line, 150 to 250pt each
  - Optional: one stat or quote spanning above or below (96 to 120pt)
  - Spacing and alignment convey credibility
  - Example: "Trusted by 50+ companies like..."

---

## Slides 19 to 23 · Implications / What To Do (5 slides)

**Pattern A: Single Concept or Keyword in Large Type**
- Use when: Implication is a new mindset or reframe
- Visual structure:
  - One word or concept in 240 to 300pt, bold
  - Background color (shift to warmer or more aspirational tone vs. argument section)
  - Minimal text; let the single concept anchor the slide
  - Example: "Ambition" or "Abundance Mindset"

**Pattern B: Action or Behavior Visual**
- Use when: Implication is "what to do" (actionable)
- Visual structure:
  - Icon or illustration representing the action (300 to 400pt)
  - Action title in 120pt next to or above icon
  - Supporting bullet (one line, 72pt) below
  - Example: Icon of a person climbing + "Take the first step"

**Pattern C: Opportunity or Aspirational Image**
- Use when: Implication is a positive future state
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed or large inspirational image (photograph or illustration)
  - Implication title overlaid in 120 to 180pt, bottom or center
  - Text is secondary; image carries the aspirational tone
  - Example: Sunrise/growth metaphor

**Pattern D: Diagram or Flowchart**
- Use when: Implication requires mapping or showing sequence
- Visual structure:
  - Simple flowchart or process diagram (not overly complex)
  - Arrows, boxes, or circles in icon-library colors
  - One main loop or cycle (max 5 steps)
  - Title in 100pt, supporting label for each step in 72pt

---

## Slides 24 to 26 · Counter-arguments & Nuance (3 slides)

**Pattern A: Objection Statement in Large Type + Contrasting Image**
- Use when: Objection is stated as a bold claim
- Visual structure:
  - Objection in 180 to 240pt, positioned left or top
  - Contrasting image or icon (300×300+) positioned opposite
  - Background color slightly muted or reserved vs. argument slides
  - Conveys seriousness and respect for the objection

**Pattern B: Split-Screen (Objection vs. Reality)**
- Use when: Visual comparison strengthens the counterargument
- Visual structure:
  - Left: objection or "what people think" with corresponding image or icon
  - Right: reality or counter-evidence with contrasting visual
  - Title spanning above or unifying the contrast
  - Bold visual difference shows the nuance

**Pattern C: Candid Quote or Vulnerability**
- Use when: Nuance is personal or experiential
- Visual structure:
  - Quote in 140 to 160pt, serif or sans-serif
  - Speaker name or context in 72pt, smaller
  - Background color or portrait photo (at 20 to 30% opacity) if speaker is sharing personally
  - Tone is honest and reflective, not defensive

**Pattern D: Data or Caveat with Visual Anchor**
- Use when: Trade-off or limit is quantified
- Visual structure:
  - Metric or statement in 180 to 240pt (e.g., "80% success rate, 20% still fail")
  - Icon or simple visual representing the dual nature
  - Supporting sentence (72pt) explaining why the caveat matters
  - Balanced tone, not doom, but realistic

---

## Slide 27 · Call To Action / Next Steps

**Pattern A: Large URL or QR Code + Action Label**
- Use when: CTA is to visit a resource or take an action online
- Visual structure:
  - QR code at 300×300 to 400×400, centered or right-aligned
  - URL in 96 to 120pt above or beside the QR code
  - Action label in 140pt (e.g., "Claim your free guide" or "Join the community")
  - Optional: icon next to action label from icon library

**Pattern B: Bold Action Statement + Contact Info**
- Use when: CTA is to connect directly or take immediate action
- Visual structure:
  - Action in 200pt bold (e.g., "Apply to the cohort" or "Share this with your team")
  - Contact method (email, calendar link, handle) in 100pt below
  - Supported by a background color or subtle icon
  - Clear and easy to remember or write down

**Pattern C: Timeline or Next Milestone**
- Use when: CTA involves a date or future event
- Visual structure:
  - Milestone or date in large type (180 to 240pt)
  - Supporting context (72pt), what happens, where to register, how to prepare
  - Optional: icon or visual marker of the event
  - Example: "Applications open March 1" with linked event visual

**Pattern D: Vision / Aspiration Paired with Action**
- Use when: CTA flows naturally from implications into a bigger call
- Visual structure:
  - Vision statement or aspiration in 160 to 200pt
  - Action step in 120pt below or next to
  - Supporting visual (icon, image, or color) that ties vision to action
  - Example: "Imagine a world where... (vision). Start today by..." (action)

---

## Slide 28 · Thank You / Contact / Closing Image

**Pattern A: Speaker Name + Contact Info + Brand Visual**
- Use when: Speaker's personal brand is prominent
- Visual structure:
  - Speaker name in 140 to 180pt, centered or top
  - Contact method (email, handle, URL) in 96pt below
  - Optional: portrait photo (200×200 to 300×300) or brand icon
  - Background color or subtle gradient
  - Matches opening aesthetic for bookend feel

**Pattern B: Full-Bleed Closing Image + Minimal Text**
- Use when: Visual closure is as important as textual
- Visual structure:
  - Full-bleed or large closing image (thematic, aspirational, or reflective of the talk)
  - Speaker name and email in 72 to 96pt, bottom corner, high contrast
  - Minimal text; image is the primary visual
  - Creates bookend with opening (if Pattern A was used)

**Pattern C: Bold Closing Statement + Icon / Symbol**
- Use when: Talk ends with a memorable phrase or call to rethink
- Visual structure:
  - Closing statement in 160 to 200pt (not "Thank you" but the actual last idea)
  - Icon or symbol at 300pt, centered or positioned asymmetrically
  - Contact in 72pt, secondary position
  - Example: "The future is built by dreamers" + lightbulb icon + email

**Pattern D: Artistic or Signature Visual Closure**
- Use when: Speaker brand is design-forward or artistic
- Visual structure:
  - Custom illustration, monogram, or signature visual at 400 to 600pt
  - Speaker name in 120pt integrated with the visual
  - Contact info in 72pt, positioned to avoid crowding the visual
  - Conveys elegance and confidence; often memorable

## Universal visual rules for all slides

- **No emoji**: All decorative symbols come from `deck-builder/references/icon-library.md`. Emoji are forbidden across every slide.
- **Text contrast**: Every text element must be readable at 10 feet (typical presentation distance). Minimum 48pt for body text, 72pt for supporting labels.
- **Image quality**: All photographs and illustrations must be high-resolution (300 DPI equivalent at presentation scale). No blurry, pixelated, or low-fidelity images.
- **Icon consistency**: Icons come from the icon library; do not invent new icons or mix from multiple libraries.
- **Color harmony**: Background colors and imagery should align with speaker brand tokens; no jarring color shifts between slides.
- **One visual anchor per slide**: Each slide has one primary visual (image, chart, icon, large type, or design element). Multiple competing visuals weaken the slide's impact.
