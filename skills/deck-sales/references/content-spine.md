# Content Spine · Sales Deck (11 slides)

This is the canonical slide structure for B2B post-discovery, pre-close sales decks. Every sales deck uses this spine as its starting point, a consultative, account-specific narrative that leads from context through proof to a specific ask. The spine reflects a 1:1 conversation with a named buyer after a discovery call.

---

### Slide 1 · Cover

**Purpose**: Establish credibility, set legal/confidentiality tone, and anchor the conversation to a specific buyer and deal context.

**Required elements**:
- Your company logo (top-left or centered, balanced sizing)
- Buyer's company name or logo (acknowledges their role)
- Deal title or meeting context (e.g., "[Buyer Company], Digital Transformation Initiative")
- Meeting date (signals when the conversation occurred; not always filled in)
- Optional: confidentiality marking if client work
- Your title and name (subtle, small type; you've already introduced yourself)

**Content density target**: Cover must feel account-specific, not generic. If swapping this cover for a different buyer, 80% of the visual should change (logo, headline, maybe company name).

**What NOT to do**:
- Generic headline that applies to any prospect (fail: "Building Better Business Together")
- Your logo significantly larger than their logo (ego-driven, reads as template)
- Blank space or decorative imagery instead of text (padding, not substance)
- Vague meeting context ("Sales Deck" instead of "Q2 Sales Process, [Account Name]")
- No date (makes the deck look recycled)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB (under 100 employees)**: Often simpler, single contact name. Cover can be less formal.
- **Enterprise (1000+)**: May have confidentiality marking, champion name, sponsoring executive. More official tone.
- **Renewal vs. New Logo**: Renewal covers may emphasize partnership longevity; new logo emphasizes fresh discovery.

**Example-filler density**: A good cover carries 5-7 distinct text elements (logo, company name, deal title, date, confidentiality if present, presenter name, meeting context) plus one visual anchor. This is dense relative to typical cover slides.

---

### Slide 2 · About You (Discovery Summary)

**Purpose**: Demonstrate that you've done deep homework on the prospect's business, market position, org structure, and recent trajectory. This is the "show you listened" slide.

**Required elements**:
- 5-7 bullets covering: industry/vertical, company stage/size (ARR, headcount, or a recent benchmark), recent major milestone (funding round, product launch, market expansion, acquisition, IPO timing), one hiring signal (especially if your solution affects that team), one public commitment or strategy announcement they've made
- One insight showing you've read recent filings, earnings calls, press releases, job postings, or founder interviews, ideally a paraphrase showing comprehension, not just facts
- NO criticism or backhanded observations ("I noticed you still use legacy systems")
- NO compliments without substance ("You're innovative")
- Visual anchor: company logo, recent product feature visual, or market position map (required; avoid leaving this all text)

**Content density target**: minimum 180-200 words of body content per slide. Each bullet should be 1-2 lines, not fragments. One bullet should be a direct quote or paraphrase from a public source, cited subtly.

**What NOT to do**:
- "You are a SaaS company", too obvious, adds zero signal
- Guessing at data instead of pulling from public sources (risks getting it wrong and looking careless)
- Mixing discovery homework with your value prop ("You're facing scale challenges, and we solve that"), that's slide 5, not here
- More than 8 bullets, starts to feel like a data dump instead of research highlights
- Visual that's unrelated or stock photo (breaks credibility)
- Reading the bullets word-for-word in the meeting (this is a confidence builder; you should have already discussed these in discovery)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Focus on recent funding, hiring spree, or customer wins. Less public data; may rely more on LinkedIn hiring signals.
- **Enterprise**: Emphasize board appointments, market strategy, acquisition activity, competitive positioning, and org restructuring.
- **Renewal context**: Emphasize achievements since the last deal closed (they've grown, hit milestones, faced new challenges).

**Example-filler density**: A strong Slide 2 contains specific, verifiable facts and one genuine insight that signals "I spent 2+ hours researching you, not 20 minutes Googling." Minimum five distinct evidence points (size, stage, recent move, hiring signal, market context).

---

### Slide 3 · The Problem (Their Pain, Their Words)

**Purpose**: Name the prospect's specific, acute pain in language THEY use (from discovery calls), not generic phrasing. Avoid the "we think you have a problem" presentation; instead, surface the cost and visibility of the problem they've already acknowledged.

**Required elements**:
- ONE primary problem (not a list of 3-5 generic challenges)
- Ideally: direct quote or close paraphrase from a discovery call or internal document you've seen
- Quantification: time waste (hours/week, person-days/year), cost leakage ($X per incident, per month, per year), error rate (%, absolute count), or growth ceiling (revenue cap, customer churn rate)
- One supporting visual: a painful workflow screenshot (e.g., manual spreadsheet with 50 columns), a trend chart showing deterioration, a process flow showing the bottleneck, or a metaphor (pile of rejected applications, duplicate database entries)
- Emotional or operational anchor: "Every missed deadline costs us a customer" or "Our team is stuck doing work that should be automated"

**Content density target**: 200+ words of narrative context. The slide should explain the problem deeply enough that someone who wasn't in discovery calls understands the scope. Example: "Right now, your team manually reconciles invoices across three systems weekly. This takes 15 hours per week, generates 8-12 reconciliation errors per month (costing ~$5K in rework), and delays payment runs by 3-5 days. Your CFO has flagged this as a compliance risk."

**What NOT to do**:
- Generic problem ("companies struggle with efficiency"), this could apply to anyone
- Problems they haven't acknowledged yet (you're trying to sell before they feel the pain)
- More than one primary problem on this slide (if there are 2+ independent problems, you need 2 slides, but that blows your slide count; instead, prioritize the most quantified one)
- All text, no visual, becomes a lecture slide; retention drops
- Using "industry research" stats instead of THEIR numbers (weaker than their own data)
- Sounding apologetic ("I know this is frustrating..."), let the data be the messenger

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Problems often center on "we're doing this manually with spreadsheets," "our team is burning out," or "we can't scale without hiring 3 more people."
- **Enterprise**: Problems are often org-wide (process fragmentation across teams, risk/compliance exposure, revenue leakage across multiple P&Ls, customer experience degradation).
- **Renewal**: Problem may evolve; frame as "when you signed up, the bottleneck was X; now you've grown and Y is the new blocker."

**Example-filler density**: Slide 3 is narrative-heavy. Expect 2-3 sentences of setup, 1 direct quote or paraphrase, 3-4 specific metrics (time, cost, error rate, or growth impact), and 1 visual showing the pain. Minimum 200 words of visible body text.

---

### Slide 4 · Why Today (Cost of Inaction, Urgency)

**Purpose**: Frame why NOW is the moment to solve this, not "someday." The urgency must be tied to THEIR business calendar or milestone, not yours.

**Required elements**:
- ONE or TWO specific business reasons (new hire quota, compliance deadline, competitive threat, market expansion, headcount freeze, system migration window, budget approval cycle)
- Quantified impact of waiting: revenue at risk over the next 6-12 months, efficiency loss if they don't act (hours compounding), growth ceiling if problem persists, risk exposure (regulatory, customer churn)
- NOT artificial urgency that benefits only you ("my quarter ends in 3 weeks")
- Visual anchor: timeline showing "today" and the consequence date with gap highlighted, cost-of-waiting card, trend projection showing deterioration, or a roadmap showing the window closing

**Content density target**: minimum 180-220 words. The logic should be ironclad: if they don't act by [date], [consequence] happens. Example: "Your hiring plan calls for 5 new senior hires in H2. At your current time-to-hire of 8 weeks, you'll miss the Q3 product launch if you don't close your recruiting process by July 1. Our solution reduces time-to-hire by 40%, which frees up 3 weeks per hire and gets you to launch on time."

**What NOT to do**:
- False urgency ("this offer expires in 30 days"), kills credibility
- Ignoring their actual blockers (they may be waiting on budget approval, not convinced of pain)
- Listing 3+ "why now" reasons, dilutes focus; pick the ONE most compelling
- No visual, becomes a slimy urgency pitch that triggers skepticism
- Creating urgency when there is none (if they have 2 years before compliance deadline, don't pretend there's an emergency)
- Urgent-sounding language without grounding ("time is of the essence" vs. "by July 1")

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Urgency often tied to "growth milestone" (we're hiring 10 more people) or "founder burnout" (founder working 80 hours/week).
- **Enterprise**: Urgency tied to budget cycles (annual procurement window closing), compliance deadlines, competitive moves, or org restructurings announced publicly.
- **Renewal**: Urgency may be "you committed to digital transformation in Q1; we're now in Q3 with inertia building; if you don't act in next 60 days, this gets deprioritized."

**Example-filler density**: Slide 4 should contain 1-2 sentences of setup, 1-2 specific business deadlines (with dates or quarters), 3-5 metrics showing cost of inaction (revenue at risk, hours compounded, customer churn risk), and 1 visual. Total visible text: 180+ words.

---

### Slide 5 · Our Solution (Plain-Language)

**Purpose**: Introduce what you do in THEIR problem context. No jargon, no feature dump, no internal terminology. Focus on capability and outcome, not mechanism.

**Required elements**:
- ONE sentence headline: "We [capability] so you can [outcome]" (e.g., "We automate invoice reconciliation so you can close financials in 1 day instead of 5")
- 5-6 bullets covering: (1) what you do in plain English, (2) who it serves / what roles benefit, (3) what you DON'T do (boundary-setting; builds trust by being honest), (4) one key differentiator (speed, accuracy, ease of use, or cost), (5) typical implementation timeline, (6) one user adoption signal (e.g., "90% of customers report using it within 2 weeks")
- Visual: product UI screenshot in the context of their use case (not a generic dashboard), before/after workflow diagram, or a capability stack visual showing what shifts
- NO feature dump ("sync from 15 systems, real-time dashboards, API, webhooks..."), focus on what changes in their day

**Content density target**: minimum 200 words visible. Each bullet should be 1-2 sentences. You're building enough narrative density that someone could understand your solution from this slide alone.

**What NOT to do**:
- Jargon overload ("leveraging ML-driven NLP to optimize multi-touch attribution"), most prospects don't know what that means
- Comparing yourself to competitors ("we're faster than X, cheaper than Y"), that's later, and only if necessary
- Over-explaining how the product works (that's Slide 6; this slide is about outcome, not mechanism)
- Generic capability ("we help teams be more efficient"), too broad
- Visual that's cluttered or out of context (a random dashboard screenshot taken from your own internal system, not contextualized to their use case)
- Talking about features instead of outcomes ("we have API access" vs. "data syncs automatically from your existing system")

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Solution must address "we can implement this without hiring engineers" or "implementation doesn't require IT approval."
- **Enterprise**: Solution must address "this integrates with systems we already own (Salesforce, SAP, etc.)" and "your security/compliance/audit team will be comfortable."
- **Renewal**: Frame evolution ("since we last worked together, we've added X and Y; here's how they unlock new value for you").

**Example-filler density**: Slide 5 is the clearest, most accessible narrative on the deck. It should contain 1 headline, 6-7 bullets (1-2 sentences each), and 1 substantial visual. Minimum 200 visible words.

---

### Slide 6 · How It Works (Product Mechanics)

**Purpose**: Walk through the actual workflow or interaction pattern. Show, don't tell. Move from "we have a solution" (Slide 5) to "here's what using it looks like."

**Required elements**:
- 4-5 step workflow clearly labeled: [Trigger] then [Step 1: Input] then [Step 2: Processing] then [Step 3: Output] then [Outcome/Value] (each step should be self-evident)
- Optional: static product screenshot sequence on this slide. Working interactive demos are a hosted feature; in the OSS pack, use a high-quality static screenshot or 3-4 numbered screenshots in sequence.
- Each step labeled in plain language, not internal jargon (not "API call" but "data feeds automatically from your Salesforce instance"; not "Kafka cluster" but "system processes reconciliation in background")
- Visuals for each step: product screenshots in sequence, workflow diagram with boxes and arrows, or step-by-step screenshots with one-line labels
- One success criterion or outcome per step (e.g., "Step 1: Reconciliation rules auto-applied, 95% match rate")

**Content density target**: minimum 150-180 words. Each step should have a label (5-8 words) plus one-line descriptor. If showing a workflow, each step visual should have a caption. If walking through screenshots in the meeting, narrate calmly and don't spend more than 2-3 minutes total on this slide.

**What NOT to do**:
- Over-engineering the diagram (10+ boxes, spaghetti arrows, technical jargon), kills clarity
- Showing internal architecture instead of user experience ("database calls Kafka cluster, spawns worker threads"), audience cares about what THEY do, not what you do
- Text-only bullets and no visual structure, becomes a lecture slide
- Screenshot that's outdated or doesn't match the current product, kills credibility instantly; pull fresh exports before the meeting
- Spending 5+ minutes on this slide, it supports the narrative, doesn't replace the ask
- Implying the process is more complex than it is (start simple; complexity comes later if they ask)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Emphasize "minimal setup; no engineering required", walk through the exact screens they'll see.
- **Enterprise**: Emphasize "integrates with your existing stack (Salesforce, SAP, etc.); your IT team controls data flow", address security/control early.
- **Renewal**: Show evolution in the workflow (e.g., "when we started, this was manual; now it's automated; next, we're adding this new step").

**Example-filler density**: Slide 6 should contain 4-5 labeled steps, 1 visual per step (screenshot, box, or icon), 1 outcome per step, and a summary statement. Minimum 150 visible words plus strong visual structure.

### Slide 7 · Proof (Named Reference Customers, Specific Outcomes)

**Purpose**: Show that this solution works for companies similar to THEM. Use named customers, specific vertical/size, quantified results. Do not rely on generic logos or vague outcomes.

**Required elements**:
- 2-3 named customer case studies (customer name, company size/vertical, specific result) OR one deep-dive case study
- Each customer MUST have: company name, vertical/size reference (e.g., "Series B SaaS, $5M ARR"), one quantified outcome (e.g., "reduced invoice reconciliation time from 15 hours/week to 2 hours/week"), ideally a quote (authentic, 1-2 sentences)
- Ideally one reference in their exact vertical (stronger signal than "we work with finance teams across all industries")
- For novel products: beta customers, pilot results, or respected advisors/investors quoted
- Visual: reference logos in a grid or row, OR detailed case study cards with outcome metrics

**Content density target**: minimum 220-280 words. A 2-3 customer proof slide should have: 2-3 customer names, 2-3 company descriptors (size, vertical, stage), 5-8 quantified outcomes total, 1-2 authentic quotes, and context on why each reference is relevant to THIS prospect.

**What NOT to do**:
- Logos without results ("we work with Fortune 500 companies"), adds zero credibility; this should be EASY to look up on your website
- Unnamed customers ("leading financial services firm"), prospect can't call them
- Vague outcomes ("increased efficiency," "improved productivity"), these could mean anything
- Comparing yourself to the prospect's competitors using their case studies (dirty; kills trust)
- No proof for novel products (if you have no customers, show beta results, pilot data, or respected advisor quotes instead)
- Logo creep (6+ logos looks like a directory of clients, not proof; pick your strongest 2-3)
- Results that don't match your pitch (if you claim 40% time savings on Slide 8 ROI, your case studies should show similar range)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Show SMB reference customers (similar size, similar constraints); enterprise references signal "this is over-built for us."
- **Enterprise**: Show enterprise references (similar size, similar org structure, similar compliance needs); SMB references signal "this is simple but not robust enough."
- **Renewal**: Emphasize how existing customers have expanded usage or achieved new outcomes since the first deal.

**Example-filler density**: Slide 7 is proof-heavy. A strong proof slide contains 2-3 customer names, 2-3 vertical/size references, 6-10 distinct quantified metrics (speed, cost, accuracy, adoption), 1-2 authentic quotes, and 1 visual. Minimum 220 words of visible text.

### Slide 8 · ROI / Value (Numbers, Not Claims)

**Purpose**: Show the financial or operational math behind the business case. Calculate, don't assert. Conservative estimates (better to beat than miss).

**Required elements**:
- Transparent inputs: price ($), implementation time (weeks, hours of their effort), expected results (time savings per user, cost reduction, or revenue impact)
- Calculation walkthrough showing how you derived the outputs (avoid "saves $1M annually" with no math shown)
- Conservative estimate (discount high-end outcomes; better to exceed than disappoint)
- 3-4 key variables shown explicitly (e.g., "20 users x 5 hours/week savings x $65/hour cost x 52 weeks = $338K annual savings"; "Annual license: $50K; Year 1 implementation cost: $15K; Total Year 1 cost: $65K; Payback period: 2.3 months")
- Visual: simple table, waterfall chart (costs, then savings, then net ROI), or side-by-side comparison (price vs. annual savings, breakeven point)

**Content density target**: minimum 240-300 words visible. This slide should justify itself with math, not marketing claims. Every number should be traceable to an assumption (number of users, hourly rates, adoption %, savings per user, etc.).

**What NOT to do**:
- Unsourced numbers ("most customers save 30% in year one"), show YOUR calculation
- Hiding the variables (the $1M claim depends on: 20 users, 5 hours/week, $60/hour rate, 50-week work year, show all of it)
- Overpromising (claiming ROI in 6 months if implementation takes 4 months)
- No visual structure, just bullet points of claims (loses credibility)
- Too many variables (more than 4-5 becomes confusing; save detailed assumptions for appendix)
- Mismatch with stage (SMB needs to see "$500K annual value" is realistic for their size; enterprise needs to see "$2M potential across 5 business units")
- Not addressing their biggest cost driver (if personnel costs are their biggest line item, ROI calculation should start there, not marginal savings)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: ROI often measured in "hours saved/week translating to FTE saved" or "days to close financial books."
- **Enterprise**: ROI measured in revenue protection (churn reduction), cost avoidance (headcount not added), or revenue acceleration (faster deal cycles).
- **Renewal**: Compare "before you signed up" to "now" to "what's possible next"; show expansion upside.

**Example-filler density**: Slide 8 should contain a complete ROI walkthrough: inputs (price, implementation effort), outputs (time savings, cost reduction), all assumptions shown, and 1 visual. Visible text: 240+ words showing the complete math.

### Slide 9 · Implementation (Timeline, Effort, Onboarding)

**Purpose**: Demystify the path from "yes" to "live." Reduce perceived risk by showing clear phases, milestones, owner assignments, and effort commitment from their side.

**Required elements**:
- Timeline in weeks: Phase 1 (Discovery/Setup, X weeks), Phase 2 (Data Migration/Config, X weeks), Phase 3 (Training, X weeks), Phase 4 (Go-live, X weeks), Phase 5 (Optimization, X weeks)
- Major milestone per phase with owner assignment (e.g., "Week 1-2: Kickoff, Your IT Lead plus Our Implementation Manager agree on data connectors")
- Resource commitment from THEIR side clearly quantified (e.g., "3 hours/week from your team lead for 4 weeks during Phase 2"; not "minimal effort")
- Your team's commitment (e.g., "our dedicated implementation manager owns the project; 1:1 weekly syncs with your lead")
- One failure mode mitigated explicitly (e.g., "We handle all data migration; your team doesn't need to modify anything in your existing systems")
- Visual: Gantt-style timeline with phases, bars showing duration, milestone markers, or swimlane diagram showing your effort vs. theirs

**Content density target**: minimum 200-250 words. Each phase should have a 1-2 sentence description, owner names or titles, and specific effort expectations.

**What NOT to do**:
- Vague timeline ("Q3" for a call in April, or "TBD"), prospects distrust vagueness
- Minimizing their effort ("just a couple hours to connect the data"), you'll lose credibility in Phase 2 when it takes longer
- No owner assigned ("someone from our team will help"), specify a name or title
- Too much detail (day-by-day task list belongs in a separate implementation playbook, not the deck)
- Hiding dependencies (if success depends on them getting IT approval by Week 2, SAY SO)
- Not addressing the biggest pain point (if their biggest concern is "will this disrupt our operations?", address that explicitly in the timeline)
- Overstating speed (if typical implementations take 8 weeks, don't promise 4 weeks to one prospect)

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Shorter timeline (typically 4-6 weeks end-to-end); emphasize "minimal IT involvement required."
- **Enterprise**: Longer timeline (typically 12-16 weeks); emphasize "we align with your change management; IT approvals built into the timeline."
- **Renewal**: Show "what's the same" (we already know your systems) and "what's new" (new integrations, new team members to onboard).

**Example-filler density**: Slide 9 should contain 5 distinct phases, 2-3 sentences per phase describing milestones, owner assignments, effort expectations from both parties, and 1 visual timeline. Minimum 200 words visible.

### Slide 10 · Pricing (Clear, No Hand-Waving)

**Purpose**: Price transparently so the budget conversation is clean. This slide must exist; it is NEVER hidden or pushed to "a follow-up call."

**Required elements**:
- Price or price range clearly stated (e.g., "$50K-$100K annually depending on user count"; "Starting at $500/month, scaling to $2000/month at 100+ users")
- What's included: number of users, data volume limits (if applicable), feature set, support level (hours, response time)
- What's NOT included (training is extra, customization work is out of scope, post-implementation support has an upsell tier)
- Comparison point: cost vs. ROI (e.g., "Annual cost: $50K; Annual savings: $338K; Year 1 payback: 1.8 months"), or cost vs. in-house build (e.g., "Building this internally would cost $500K+ in engineering time"), or cost vs. market benchmark
- Optional: payment terms (annual vs. month-to-month, upfront discount, implementation fee separate), contract length (1-year, 3-year, no lock-in)
- Visual: price card(s) with inclusions/exclusions, comparison table, or ROI ratio display

**Content density target**: minimum 180-220 words. Every number should be transparent. Include a table or card showing exactly what's included and at what volume/usage levels pricing changes.

**What NOT to do**:
- "Let's talk pricing after you decide", prospects hate it; it flags you're hiding something
- Hidden costs ("implementation is an extra $20K"), emerges in negotiation, kills deal trust
- Mismatch with ROI (if Slide 8 claims $338K annual value, pricing at $150K/year is defensible; pricing at $500K/year makes the ROI claim look inflated)
- Too many tiers (keep to 2-3 options max on the deck; 6+ tiers overwhelm)
- Vague language ("affordable," "competitive," "custom pricing"), use actual numbers
- Not addressing their budget constraints (if this is a $50K deal and you're pricing at $150K, flag the gap; maybe a phased approach or smaller pilot makes sense)
- Assuming they know what "per seat" or "per transaction" means, explain it clearly

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Pricing often per-user or per-month; mention month-to-month flexibility or annual discount.
- **Enterprise**: Pricing often per-user on a volume curve, with custom support SLA; mention implementation costs are separate and usually 20-40% of Year 1 value.
- **Renewal**: Show how pricing scales as they grow; "you started at 10 users ($5K/year), now you're at 50 users ($15K/year); next you can go to 100 users at $25K/year."

**Example-filler density**: Slide 10 should contain the exact price or price range, a bulleted list of inclusions/exclusions, one comparison (vs. ROI, vs. in-house build, vs. competitor if applicable), and 1 visual. Minimum 180 words of visible text, with numbers paramount.

### Slide 11 · Next Steps (Specific, Calendar-Ready)

**Purpose**: Walk out of the meeting with a clear, committed next action. Names, dates, and calendar links are mandatory.

**Required elements**:
- Named human: "Sarah, our VP of Sales, will send you a calendar invite for Thursday, April 25 at 2pm PT" (not "someone from our team will follow up")
- Specific date and time OR a calendar link/QR code (if digital deck, make the link clickable)
- What will happen in that meeting: "30-minute technical proof-of-concept where we'll walk through your specific workflows"
- One clear ask/question for them: "Can you confirm by end of day Wednesday if Sarah's Thursday time works? If not, we'll find a backup slot Friday morning."
- Optional: what you'll prepare for them before the meeting (e.g., "we'll send you a draft implementation timeline by tomorrow morning so you have it before the call")
- Visual: centered card with action detail, or a commitment matrix if there are multiple follow-up actions with different owners

**Content density target**: minimum 150-180 words. Even though this is the final slide, it should be substantive: "Next Steps" is not a single bullet point; it's a small project plan.

**What NOT to do**:
- Generic closings ("let's stay in touch," "we'll be in touch soon"), these are conversation enders, not calls to action
- No date ("we'll follow up next week"), too vague; "next week" means different things
- No owner ("someone from our team will call you"), commit a specific person
- Asking for too much at once (decision plus approval plus budget review plus IT sign-off in one conversation)
- CTA that's not from the person in the room (if you're the one pitching, you own the follow-up; don't push it to "sales ops will call")
- Not addressing the mutual next step (what do THEY need to do? Review something? Get approvals? Gather their team for the POC?)
- Leaving without an explicit agreement ("let me know if this doesn't work for you" is weaker than "can you give me a yes/no by EOD today?")

**Stage-specific adjustments**:
- **SMB**: Often a single next meeting with the owner; one person, one decision path.
- **Enterprise**: May have multiple parallel workstreams (technical POC with IT, budget discussion with finance, use-case validation with ops); each needs an owner and a date.
- **Renewal**: Next step may be "let's set up a joint roadmap planning session; I'll invite Sarah (your success manager) and Mike (our product lead) to align on Year 2 priorities."

**Example-filler density**: Slide 11 should contain 1-2 named contacts from your company, 1-2 specific dates/times, 1-2 explicit agenda items for the next meeting, 1-2 action items for THEM to complete before that meeting, and 1 visual or centered text card. Minimum 150 words visible.

## Hard constraints

**Slide count**: 10 to 12 slides (11 is the target; 10 is lean and sharp, 12 means one slide is redundant). The slide count MUST match the brief's declared target of 11.

**Reading time**: 45 seconds per slide maximum (11 slides equals ~8 minutes of presentation time; prospect should feel heard and informed, not lectured or overwhelmed).

**No emojis**: Use only icon-library.md SVG icons; no Unicode emoji characters.

**Typography scale declared in CSS**: The first line of the deck's `<style>` block MUST include a comment declaring the scale, e.g. `/* Typography scale (7 sizes): 10px, 14px, 18px, 24px, 32px, 48px, 56px */`. The Layout Reviewer enforces that every `font-size` value in the file matches one of the declared sizes, and that the count is less than or equal to 7. This prevents the "13 distinct font sizes, no declared scale" failure mode.

**No hidden-content filler**: Zero `display: none`, `visibility: hidden`, `opacity: 0`, or `height: 0` declarations outside the two base `.slide` rules. If the deck is short of the 60K file-size floor, add REAL VISIBLE CONTENT across slides. Do NOT add hidden blocks to pad bytes. Preferred pattern: use `.slide { opacity: 0 }` plus `.slide.active { opacity: 1 }` for transitions; this makes the anti-gaming check trivial (zero `display: none` in the file).

**No inline `justify-content: center` on `<section class="slide">`**: Centering an 810px flex column causes content-taller-than-canvas to overflow symmetrically above the top padding, hiding behind the absolute-positioned header zone. If a slide needs centered content (cover, closing), use a dedicated CSS class (`.s-cover`, `.s-closing`), never inline style or class-less section.

**Every ROI/value claim is sourced**: case study, customer result, or benchmark; never an unsourced assertion (e.g., "most customers save 30%"). Claim must be traceable to a named customer on Slide 7 or a verifiable public benchmark.

**Pricing slide is NEVER omitted or hidden**: Slide 10 must exist and must state price (or "TBD" if pricing is genuinely in discussion) plus inclusions/exclusions plus one comparison. If pricing is TBD, state that plainly on the slide rather than avoiding the slide entirely.

**Next Steps slide names specific humans and dates**: Slide 11 must include a named human (title and name, or at least title), a specific date or calendar link, and an explicit ask. No generic "we'll follow up"; must be actionable from the meeting room.

**Proof slide must reference NAMED customers**: Slide 7 must include customer names and company descriptors (vertical, size). Unnamed references ("a leading financial services firm") fail the credibility check. If you have no named customers, show beta results, pilot data, or respected advisor quotes instead.

**If competition is mentioned, be honest**: No invented weaknesses or strawman comparisons. Credibility beats aggressive positioning. If you claim "competitors can't do X," you must have evidence; otherwise, drop the comparison.
